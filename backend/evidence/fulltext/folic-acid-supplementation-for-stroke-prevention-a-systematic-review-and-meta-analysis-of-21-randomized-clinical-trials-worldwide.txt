Background & aims: The AHA/ASA guidelines for primary stroke prevention are almost a decade old. The
current recommendation regarding folic acid supplementation is based on only 8 clinical trials, and an
additional 13 folate trials have been published since then. This meta-analysis aims to ﬁll in critical ev-
idence gaps by comprehensively evaluating 21 published trials with particular attention given to iden-
tifying the true inﬂuences through stratiﬁcation.
Methods: PubMed, the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials, and Embase were searched from
inception to April 4, 2023. This study included all randomized controlled trials (RCTs) of folic acid with
stroke as one of the reporting endpoints. Relative risks and 95% conﬁdence intervals were used to assess
the association between folic acid supplementation and the risk of stroke in a random-effects model.
Results: Results from the 21 pooled RCTs totaling 115,559 participants showed that folic acid supple-
mentation signiﬁcantly reduced the risk of stroke by 10% (RR 0.90, 95%CI 0.83 to 0.98). Subgroup analyses
showed that folic acid efﬁcacy was greater in areas without fortiﬁed grain or with partially-fortiﬁed grain
(RR ¼ 0.83, 95% CI 0.75 to 0.93; RR ¼ 1.04 in areas with grain fortiﬁcation, P-interaction ¼ 0.003). In this
group, folic acid supplementation was most efﬁcacious in those without a history of stroke or myocardial
infarction (RR ¼ 0.77, 95% CI 0.68 to 0.86; RR ¼ 0.94 for participants with a history of stroke or
myocardial infarction, P-interaction ¼ 0.008). The efﬁcacy of folic acid remained consistent regardless of
baseline folate levels, folic acid dosage, baseline vitamin B12 levels, vitamin B12 dosage, homocysteine
reduction, intervention duration, and whether folic acid was taken alone or in combination (all P-
interaction>0.05). All 21 trials were free of attrition bias and reporting bias, and there was no signiﬁcant
publication bias.
Conclusions: This is by far the largest meta-analysis of RCTs regarding folic acid supplementation and
stroke, demonstrating the overall beneﬁt of folic acid for stroke prevention. Grain fortiﬁcation and his-
tory of stroke or myocardial infarction may be the most important inﬂuences on the efﬁcacy of folic acid
for stroke prevention.

The efﬁcacy of folic acid for stroke prevention has been debated
for almost 30 years. There is an urgent need to resolve the debate in
light of the high population burden of stroke and its associated
morbidity and mortality [1], as well as the growing evidence that
folate is a simple, safe, and effective intervention for stroke pre-
vention [2,3]. The AHA/ASA guidelines for the primary prevention
of stroke are ten years old, and made the class IIb recommendation

on folic acid supplementation for stroke prevention (level of evi-
dence B) [4]. The most recent high-quality meta-analysis of folic
acid and stroke is seven years old [5], and there have been addi-
tional folate trials published since then. In 2022, the US Preventive
Services Task Force (USPSTF) concluded that the current evidence
was insufﬁcient to assess the beneﬁts and harms of the use of
multivitamin supplements, or single or paired nutrient supple-
ments for the prevention of cardiovascular disease (Grade I state-
ment) [6]. However, the USPSTF report treated cardiovascular
disease as a single entity and ignored substantial evidence on folic
acid supplementation in stroke prevention. There is increasing
recognition that there are signiﬁcant differences in the efﬁcacy of
folic acid supplementation for stroke vs myocardial infarction [3],
or other types of cardiovascular disease, and between primary vs
secondary prevention of stroke [7].
Furthermore, according to the AHA/ASA stroke prevention
guidelines, folic acid supplementation was found to be more
effective in trials with treatment durations exceeding 3 years,
where plasma homocysteine concentrations decreased by over
20%, in regions without folic acid fortiﬁcation, and among partici-
pants with no history of stroke [4]. Yet, these conclusions relied on
data from just 8 trials, while an additional 13 large trials have been
published in the decade following. Subsequent meta-analyses have
introduced new perspectives, suggesting that combined supple-
mentation with high-dose cyanocobalamin might be detrimental in
patients with impaired renal function, potentially explaining the
limited beneﬁts observed in previous trials [2,7]. While insightful,
these studies have methodological limitations. Most clinical trials
of high-dose cyanocobalamin supplementation in renal-impaired
patients were conducted in grain-fortiﬁed areas. However, it is
the presence or absence of folic acid fortiﬁcation, not the cyano-
cobalamin dosage, that primarily drives the outcomes. Stratifying
by key inﬂuential factors and then conducting subsequent explo-
rations within each stratum may yield more reliable conclusions.
Through methodical evaluation of all 21 trials available to date
(N ¼ 115,559), this systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to
ﬁll in critical evidence gaps and identify the true factors that affect
the effectiveness of folic acid through comprehensive stratiﬁcation.
Findings from this meta-analysis will provide high-quality, high-
level evidence on the role of folic acid supplementation in stroke
primary prevention. Such evidence is critically needed to resolve
the long-standing controversy and to inform relevant clinical
guidelines.
1. Methods
1.1. Search methods and resources
We conducted a comprehensive and detailed literature search of
PubMed, the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CEN-
TRAL) and Embase from inception to April 4, 2023, using the
following terms: “stroke”, “cerebrovascular accident”, “cardiovas-
cular
disease”,
“coronary
disease”,
“coronary
thrombosis”,
“myocardial ischemia”, “coronary stenosis”, “coronary restenosis”,
“folic
acid”,
“multivitamins”,
“5-methyltetrahydrofolate”
and
“folate”. Searches were restricted to human studies and random-
ized controlled trials. There were no language restrictions. Manual
searches of bibliographies of all relevant trials and review articles
were also performed. Independent PubMed, CENTRAL and Embase
searches were conducted by NZ and ZZ, using identical search
methods. We also consulted with experts in the ﬁeld.
1.2. Study selection
The study inclusion criteria were as follows: (1) the study was a
randomized, controlled clinical trial; (2) the number of stroke
events occurring during the study period was reported in both the
intervention and control groups and the total number of stroke
events exceeded ten; (3) the study intervention included folic acid
supplementation (with or without B vitamins and multivitamin
supplementation); and (4) the duration of the study intervention
was at least 6 months.
Study selection was performed by a study team according to a
standard protocol. The study team consisted of experts in clinical
medicine, clinical trials, epidemiology, and statistics. A total of 2006
reports were retrieved, and the relevant abstract content was
independently reviewed by two investigators (NZ and ZZ). When
discrepancies arose, a third investigator (XC) completed an inde-
pendent
assessment.
Any
discrepancies
in
judgments
were
resolved through joint discussion among the study team. The se-
lection process has been summarized using a ﬂow diagram
(Supplementary Fig. 1).
1.3. Data extraction
The entire data extraction process used pre-designed standard
forms. According to standard protocol, all data were extracted
independently in duplicate by two independent investigators (ZZ
and NZ) and reviewed independently by a third investigator (XC).
Discrepancies were resolved through discussion by the multidis-
ciplinary research team that developed the standard protocol.
The information extracted from each eligible trial included the
following: study name if available, ﬁrst author's name, year of
publication, study design (factorial, parallel, crossover, other), type
of blinding (open, double blind), type of controls (placebo, usual
care, untreated), study population, country of origin, grain fortiﬁ-
cation status, total number of participants, total number of strokes
in the treatment and control groups, intervention regimen, dura-
tion of intervention, homocysteine concentration in the treatment
and control groups (baseline and the last visit), mean age, per-
centage male, and other baseline characteristics.
Net reduction in homocysteine was calculated as the change in
concentration of the intervention group minus the change in con-
centration of the control group. For randomized controlled trials
with a factorial design, all participants who received folic acid
supplementation were compared with those in the placebo group,
regardless of the intervention of other B vitamins [8,9]. For studies
in which all participants were supplemented with different doses
of folic acid, the group with the lowest dose of folic acid inter-
vention was used as the control group [10e12]. Countries with folic
acid grain fortiﬁcation policies included the United States, Canada,
Australia and New Zealand. As Australia and New Zealand are
countries with voluntary folic acid fortiﬁcation, in this meta-
analysis, these areas were considered as areas with grain fortiﬁ-
cation [13]. More than 85% of the participants in the FAVORIT study
were from the United States and Canada, and in this meta-analysis,

1.4. Methodological quality assessment
Two investigators (ZZ and NZ) independently assessed the risk
of bias for all randomized controlled trials, including selection bias,
performance bias, detection bias, attrition bias, reporting bias, and
other biases, as assessed by the Cochrane risk-of-bias algorithm
[15,16]. Seven criteria were evaluated, with the bias risk for each
rated as low, high, or unclear. Any discrepancies were resolved
through discussion.
1.5. Statistical analysis
The outcome of interest was total stroke, including both
ischemic and hemorrhagic types. We evaluated the overall effect of
folic acid supplementation on stroke risk based on all data from all
21 trials to date. Relative risk (RR) with 95% conﬁdence interval (CI)
was used as a measure of the association between folic acid sup-
plementation and risk of stroke. Although the results of the tests of
heterogeneity for both the main and the stratiﬁed analyses were
not signiﬁcant (with I2 less than 50%), after a group discussion, our
ﬁnal conclusion was that the potential clinical heterogeneity across
studies could not be ignored, and that it was more appropriate to
choose a random-effects model [17]. All P values were from two-
sided tests and results were considered statistically signiﬁcant at
P < 0.05.
We comprehensively reviewed studies on folic acid supple-
mentation for stroke prevention, including clinical trials, meta-
analyses, and reviews, and ultimately identiﬁed potential sources
of clinical heterogeneity, including folic acid fortiﬁcation, primary
vs secondary prevention, folic acid dosage, vitamin B12 dosage,
baseline folic acid and vitamin B12 levels, duration of intervention
and homocysteine lowering.
Further stratiﬁed analyses were conducted by grain fortiﬁcation
(yes vs no/partial); history of stroke or myocardial infarction (yes vs
no); folic acid supplementation alone (yes vs no); baseline folate
(<16.0 nmol/L vs 16.0 nmol/L); folic acid dosage (0.8 mg/d vs
>0.8 mg/d); baseline vitamin B12 (<431.4 pg/mL vs >431.4 pg/mL);
vitamin B12 dosage (<0.4 mg/d vs 0.4 mg/d); decrease in homo-
cysteine concentration (<20% vs 20%) and duration of folic acid
supplementation (36 months vs >36 months). Random-effects
models were used in stratiﬁed analyses. Since the inclusion of tri-
als of multivitamin supplementation could be a potential source of
methodological
heterogeneity,
we
effectively
addressed
this
through sensitivity analyses.
Heterogeneity was assessed through the Cochrane's Q test and
inconsistency index (I2) with a signiﬁcance level set at 0.10.
Sensitivity analyses were performed by removing each individual
trial from the meta-analysis to evaluate the stability of the pooled
RR. Publication bias was also assessed by the Begg's funnel plot and
the Peters test, where an asymmetric funnel plot with P < 0.05
indicated the possibility of publication bias. R software, version
4.2.0 (http://www.R-project.org/), was used for all statistical
analyses.

consents
This meta-analysis follows the Preferred Reporting Items for
Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) statement [18].
The protocol was registered with PROSPERO (CRD42022383737).

3. Results
A total of 2006 reports were retrieved. After reviewing the ab-
stracts, 1891 reports were excluded, including those that were not
randomized controlled trials, those that had no cardiovascular
disease endpoints, and those with no folic acid intervention. A total
of 49 protocols and registration information were also excluded. Of
the 66 studies evaluated in detail, 45 studies were excluded due to
duplicate reporting (35), lack of data on stroke or cerebrovascular
endpoints (6), and low numbers of stroke cases (4). Ultimately, a
total of 21 clinical trials, including a total of 115,559 participants
and 4129 stroke cases, were included in this meta-analysis
(Supplementary Fig. 1).
3.1. Basic characteristics of the included clinical trials
Table 1 presents the baseline characteristics of the study par-
ticipants, while Table 2 outlines the design characteristics of the
included studies. The sample sizes of the clinical trials ranged from
88 to 21,442 participants. Of the total 21 trials, 8 were conducted in
or primarily in the United States and Canada [10,11,14,19e23],1 was
conducted in Australia and New Zealand [13], 8 were conducted in
European countries [8,9,12,24e28], 2 were conducted in China
[29,30], and 2 were conducted in multiple countries and regions
[31,32]. Of these, 9 studies were conducted in grain-fortiﬁed re-
gions [10,11,13,14,19e23], 10 in regions with no grain fortiﬁcation
[8,9,12,24e27,29,30,33], and 2 in regions with partial grain fortiﬁ-
cation [31,32]. Most trials enrolled populations with pre-existing
conditions, including stroke, end-stage renal disease, myocardial
infarction (MI), and hypertension. The folic acid supplementation
dosage in the trial intervention groups ranged from 0.4 mg to 40 mg
per day, with durations ranging from 24 to 134.4 months. In trials
with combined vitamin B12 supplementation, the vitamin B12
dosage in the intervention group varied from 6 mg to 2 mg per day.
3.2. Efﬁcacy of folic acid supplementation for stroke prevention
Pooling the data from all 21 clinical trials revealed a signiﬁcant
10% reduction in the risk of stroke with folic acid supplementation
(RR 0.90, 95% CI 0.83 to 0.98; Fig. 1); the test for heterogeneity did
not show signiﬁcant variation (P ¼ 0.100). A sensitivity analysis
conﬁrmed that the results did not substantially alter even after
excluding any single trial (Supplementary Fig. 2).

for stroke risk by history of stroke or mi in populations with different
grain fortiﬁcation status
In populations with grain fortiﬁcation, folic acid supplementa-
tion did not reduce the risk of stroke (RR 1.04, 95% CI 0.94 to 1.14;

3.6. Methodological quality assessment
Of the 21 included trials, three trials did not report the speciﬁc
method of random sequence generation and were assessed as un-
certain risk of selection bias. Five trials did not describe the process
of allocation concealment and were assessed as uncertain risk. Two
trials were designed to be open-label and were assessed as high
risk of performance bias. Seven trials were assessed as having an
unclear risk of detection bias because it was unclear whether
blinding was used in the outcome assessment. All 21 trials were
free
of
attrition
bias,
reporting
bias,
and
other
biases
(Supplementary Figs. 3 and 4).
No signiﬁcant differences were found in the efﬁcacy of folic acid
supplementation for stroke prevention when the results were
stratiﬁed according to methodological quality (Supplementary
Table 1).
The Begg's funnel plot was symmetrical (Supplementary Fig. 5)
and the results of the Peters test showed no signiﬁcant publication
bias for the 21 trials included in this study (P ¼ 0.152). We also
compared studies of large sample size to those of small sample size
in order to further assess publication bias, and found that the re-
sults remained consistent (Supplementary Table 2).
4. Discussion
This is by far the largest meta-analysis of folic acid supple-
mentation for stroke prevention, based on 21 clinical trials with a
total of 115,559 participants. The ﬁndings support the beneﬁt of
folic acid supplementation for stroke prevention. Speciﬁcally, we
validated the four risk factors mentioned in the AHA/ASA guide-
lines and ultimately conﬁrmed that only folic acid fortiﬁcation
status and history of stroke or myocardial infarction (MI) were the
top two inﬂuences, with homocysteine reduction rate and inter-
vention duration no longer being signiﬁcant. Of greater importance,
after fully considering the contribution of the ﬁrst two factors
(which had been overlooked in previous studies), the other inﬂu-
encing factors identiﬁed by the previous guidelines and meta-
analysis, no longer appear particularly important. Individuals
without a history of stroke or MI in non-grain fortiﬁed areas
maximized the beneﬁts of folic acid supplementation, experiencing
a signiﬁcant 23% reduction in stroke risk. Below we discuss our

In 1998, the United States and Canada implemented a manda-
tory food fortiﬁcation program that added 140 ug of folic acid to
every 100 g of grain [34]. This initiative signiﬁcantly increased
circulating folate levels and reduced homocysteine concentrations
in the population within two years, with the prevalence of high
homocysteine concentrations dropping from 18.7% to 9.8% in
middle-aged and older US adults [35]. The dramatic impact of
mandatory folic acid grain fortiﬁcation policies may have weakened
the statistical power of relevant clinical trials from the US and
Canada [36,37]. The presence or absence of grain fortiﬁcation
serves as a determinant of folate clinical trials. This study yielded
consistent results: folic acid supplementation signiﬁcantly reduced
stroke risk in trials conducted in regions without grain fortiﬁcation
or with partial grain fortiﬁcation (RR: 0.83; 95% CI: 0.75 to 0.93). In
contrast, no beneﬁt of folic acid was observed in trials conducted in
grain fortiﬁed

subpopulations with low folic acid intake or metabolic folate
insufﬁciency, as indicated by low blood folate levels or elevated
homocysteine. As a key gene in folate metabolism, the MTHFR
677C/T polymorphism has been associated with elevated homo-
cysteine levels and an increased risk of stroke, particularly in
populations with low folate levels [38]. Recent evidence suggests
that, compared to the CC genotype, the TT genotype is associated
with a higher risk of both ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke [39].
Focusing on those populations with low folate, high homocysteine,
and high stroke risk might be an important direction for future
research.

In recent years, several studies have concluded that high-dose
cyanocobalamin supplementation in patients with renal impair-
ment may lead to adverse effects, explaining the lack of signiﬁcant
beneﬁts observed in previous clinical trials [2,40]. However, these
trials of high-dose cyanocobalamin supplementation were con-
ducted in regions with grain fortiﬁcation. We speculate that it is the
presence or absence of folic acid fortiﬁcation, rather than the
dosage of cyanocobalamin, that acts as the primary driving force. To
validate our theory, we ﬁrst stratiﬁed the studies according to
whether they were populations from grain-fortiﬁed regions or non-
grain-fortiﬁed region. We found that in regions without grain
fortiﬁcation, the efﬁcacy of folic acid supplementation for stroke
primary prevention remained noteworthy even with the addition
of high-dose cyanocobalamin supplementation. Indeed, for in-
dividuals with low baseline vitamin B12 levels, including vegetar-
ians, patients with severe gastrointestinal disorders, and elderly
diabetic patients on long-term metformin use [33], it might be
worth considering supplementing with the appropriate form and
dose of vitamin B12 as a preventive measure against stroke [41].
Unfortunately, drawing similar conclusions based on the available
evidence is not feasible since baseline B12 levels in existing clinical
trials fall within the normal range.
In addition to grain fortiﬁcation, cardiovascular history, and
cyanocobalamin dose, prior meta-analyses have proposed various
factors that might impact the effectiveness of folic acid supple-
mentation in stroke prevention, although these results have been
inconsistent. These additional factors include the duration of the
intervention, whether folic acid was supplemented alone or with
other nutrients, the dose of folic acid supplementation, the per-
centage decrease in homocysteine, as well as baseline levels of folic
acid and vitamin B12 [40,42,43]. However, after a comprehensive
stratiﬁed analysis, none of these factors appeared to have signiﬁ-
cant importance (all P-interaction > 0.05). In comparison to the
AHA/ASA stroke prevention guidelines [4], this study reafﬁrms that
grain fortiﬁcation and a history of stroke or myocardial infarction
are the top two determinants inﬂuencing the efﬁcacy of folic acid
for stroke prevention. Intervention duration and percentage of
homocysteine reduction no longer appear to be signiﬁcant ac-
cording to the available evidence. Stratifying by key inﬂuential
factors and conducting subsequent explorations within each stra-
tum have enabled us to identify the true inﬂuences on folic acid
efﬁcacy, thereby addressing a longstanding controversy.
The AHA/ASA guidelines for the primary prevention of stroke,
which have not been updated for a decade, suggested that folic acid
might be considered for the prevention of stroke, but its effec-
tiveness has not been well established (Class of recommendation:
IIb; level of evidence: B) [4]. The guidelines were based on evidence
from 8 clinical trials (16,841 individuals), and an additional 13
clinical trials (98,718 individuals) have been published since then.
In 2022, a US Preventive Services Task Force report concluded that
the current evidence is insufﬁcient to assess the beneﬁts and harms
of the use of multivitamin supplements, or single or paired nutrient
supplements for the prevention of cardiovascular disease (Grade I
statement) [6,44]. Cardiovascular disease was treated as a single
entity, again ignoring the large body of evidence related to folic acid
for stroke prevention. The evidence showed that folic acid sup-
plementation did not signiﬁcantly impact the risk of coronary heart
disease (RR: 1.04, 95% CI: 0.99 to 1.09) [5]. Similarly, another meta-
analysis concluded that folic acid supplementation did not reduce
the risk of myocardial infarction (RR: 1.24, 95% CI: 0.87 to 1.75) [45].
The clear stroke beneﬁt of folic acid should not be confused with
the relationship between folic acid and other cardiovascular out-
comes. This meta-analysis, treating stroke as an independent
endpoint and excluding the effects of other cardiovascular end-
points, comprehensively reviewed all the evidence to date. We
believe that we now have sufﬁcient evidence to recommend folic
acid for primary prevention of stroke.

5. Limitations
This study has some limitations. First, being a meta-analysis, it
comes with inherent limitations, including aggregation, its retro-
spective nature, and the inability to comprehensively collect and
adjust for individual characteristics. Second, publication bias is a
common concern in meta-analyses. We evaluated publication bias
using funnel plots, which did not indicate any signiﬁcant bias.
Third, the primary endpoint in many of the relevant clinical trials
was primarily cardiovascular disease rather than stroke. Addition-
ally, only three trials reported both ischemic and hemorrhagic
stroke types, thus limiting our ability to explore the effects of folic
acid supplementation on different stroke types. Fourth, in some
trials, mean baseline folate and vitamin B12 levels were not re-
ported, which could potentially introduce bias when stratifying by
folate and vitamin B12. Finally, in an effort to gain a more
comprehensive understanding of the factors affecting the efﬁcacy
of folic acid, our study needed to include a broader spectrum of
trials with larger sample sizes. Four trials using multiple nutrient
supplements (including folic acid) were also included in this meta-
analysis. However, after removing the four trials, the sensitivity
analysis showed consistent results.
6. Conclusions
This is by far the largest meta-analysis of RCTs of folic acid
supplementation in stroke prevention to date, demonstrating the
overall beneﬁt of folic acid for stroke prevention and even greater
beneﬁt among the subgroup without folic acid fortiﬁcation and
without a history of stroke or myocardial infarction.
