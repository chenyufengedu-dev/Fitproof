A B S T R A C T
Background: Weekend catch-up sleep (WCS) may alleviate weekday sleep deprivation, but its relationship with 
depression risk remains unclear. This systematic review and meta-analysis aims to explore the association be­
tween WCS and depression risk.
Methods: A comprehensive search was conducted in PubMed, Cochrane Library, Embase, Web of Science, and 
Scopus for observational studies published up to June 1, 2024. Data extraction and bias assessment were 
independently performed by two reviewers. Odds ratios (ORs) and 95 % confidence intervals (CIs) were 
calculated, with model selection based on the I2 statistic. Sensitivity analyses and publication bias tests were also 
conducted.
Results: A total of ten cross-sectional studies (326,871 participants) were included. Meta-analysis showed that 
WCS was significantly associated with a reduced risk of depression (OR = 0.80, 95 % CI: 0.68–0.90). Subgroup 
analyses showed moderate amounts of WCS (0–2 h) may be protective, but WCS beyond 2 h had limited pro­
tective effects against depression. Qualitative analyses showed that the protective effect of WCS against 
depression was more pronounced in men and middle-aged adults, and was particularly applicable to those who 
were sleep-deprived on weekdays.
Limitations: The cross-sectional design of included studies limits causality inference, and the sample primarily 
represents populations from the United States and South Korea, potentially affecting generalizability.
Conclusions: Moderate WCS is associated with lower depression risk in those with weekday sleep deficits, while 
excessive WCS may have diminishing or adverse effects. Further research should examine optimal WCS duration 
and underlying mechanisms.
1. Background
Depression is the most common mental disorder and has become a 
public health problem of wide concern globally, with serious implica­
tions for individuals, families, and society (Liu et al., 2024a, 2024b). 
Depression is usually characterized by negative mood, weight changes, 
cognitive changes, poor quality of life, and increased risk of suicide, 
which not only affects an individual's mental health but also triggers a 
series of physiological problems such as decreased immune function and 
cardiovascular disease (Harshfield et al., 2020). The World Health Or­
ganization (WHO) estimates that >300 million people are affected by 
depression globally, and this number continues to rise, with lifetime 
prevalence rates ranging from 15 % to 18 %, and suicide rates as high as 
15 % (WHO, 2023). While there are various ways to treat major 
depression, unfortunately, around 30 % of individuals who undergo two 
or more sessions remain unrelieved (Chaudhri et al., 2024; Komariah 
et al., 2022). Therefore, the prevention of depression is of irreplaceable 
importance. Numerous studies have shown that there is a close associ­
ation between sleep and mental health, and in particular, the relation­
ship between sleep deprivation and depression has been widely 
confirmed (Hill et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022).
In the fast-paced life of modern society, many people often lack 
sufficient sleep during weekdays, resulting in lower work efficiency, 
increased mood swings, and even symptoms of depression (Hu et al., 
2021). To compensate for the lack of sleep on weekdays, many people 
catch up on sleep on weekends, a particularly prevalent phenomenon 
among adolescents and adults (Choi et al., 2023). Weekend catch-up 
sleep (WCS), i.e., compensating for weekday sleep deprivation by 
sleeping longer on weekends, is an effective way to recover from fatigue 
(Hirshkowitz et al., 2015). Implementing such measures may mitigate

the negative consequences associated with sleep indebtedness, such as 
obesity, metabolic syndrome, and cardiovascular disease (Im et al., 
2017; Jang et al., 2023; Son et al., 2020). However, the effects of WCS on 
mental health, especially the association with depression, have not been 
fully studied and concluded. Some studies suggest that WCS may help 
alleviate fatigue and mood problems caused by sleep deprivation, 
thereby improving mental health to some extent (Kitamura et al., 2016). 
For example, some studies have found that catching up on sleep over the 
weekend may improve mood states and reduce anxiety and depressive 
symptoms (Larson and Gehrman, 2024). However, other studies present 
a different perspective. They argue that WCS may disrupt the biological 
clock and lead to disrupted sleep rhythms, which can exacerbate 
depressive symptoms (Akerstedt et al., 2019). Chronic sleep irregularity 
may negatively affect brain function and the nervous system, which in 
turn increases the risk of depression (Depner et al., 2019). Additionally, 
the effects of catching up on sleep over the weekend may vary depending 
on individual differences. Factors such as an individual's sleep needs, 
lifestyle, and health status may influence the effectiveness of WCS. Some 
studies have found that younger people and those with more severe 
sleep deprivation may benefit more from WCS, whereas for those with 
less severe sleep problems or more sensitive biological clocks, WCS may 
have negative effects (Yoshiike et al., 2023).
Although existing studies provide some preliminary evidence on the 
relationship between WCS and depression, there are some differences in 
the results of these studies, which may be related to factors such as the 
study population, methodology, and sample size. Therefore, it is 
necessary to conduct a systematic review and meta-analysis of existing 
studies to comprehensively assess the effects of WCS on depression. A 
systematic review and meta-analysis may reveal whether WCS is indeed 
effective in alleviating depressive symptoms or whether it may lead to 
more serious psychological problems. Additionally, such an analysis 
could help identify which groups may benefit most from WCS and which 
groups may require different sleep management strategies. Ultimately, 
this will help develop more precise health interventions and provide a 
scientific basis for improving the public's sleep quality and mental health 
and reducing the burden of depression.
2. Methods
This systematic review was conducted and reported according to the 
Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses 
(PRISMA) guidelines (Page et al., 2021). The methodology of this 
study is registered on the PROSPERO website (registration number: 
CRD42024553471).
2.1. Search strategy
PubMed, Cochrane Library, Embase, Web of Science, and Scopus 
were searched for observational studies published from the database's 
inception to June 1, 2024. There were no language restrictions and the 
search strategy combined the use of medical subject headings (MeSH) 
and keywords. Search terms included weekend catch-up sleep, weekend 
recovery sleep, catch-up sleep, sleep compensation, sleep debt recovery, 
weekend sleep extension, sleep rebound, sleep patterns, depression, 
depressive, depression disorder, dysthymia, mood disorder, and affec­
tive disorder. The full search strategy using Pubmed as an example is 
included in Appendix Fig. A.1. References of included studies were also 
examined to identify relevant studies.
2.2. Eligibility criteria
Inclusion criteria: (1) Type of study: observational studies, including 
but not limited to cohort studies, cross-sectional studies, etc.; (2) Sub­
jects: can include individuals of any age, sex, and demographic char­
acteristics, but must explicitly report WCS behavior as well as their 
depressive symptoms; (3) Content of the study: a study on the 
correlation between WCS and depression; and (4) Depression or 
depressive symptoms as an outcome variable.
Exclusion criteria: (1) reviews, research protocols, editorials, and 
conference papers; (2) inaccessibility of full text; and (3) failure to 
provide complete or detailed information.
2.3. Study selection
Study selection was conducted by two reviewers who independently 
screened the literature based on eligibility and exclusion criteria. 
Duplicate and irrelevant articles were first excluded based on title and 
abstract. Subsequently, the full text of potentially eligible articles was 
downloaded and read to identify all eligible studies. Any disagreements 
were resolved by the third reviewer, who acted as an arbiter.
2.4. Data extraction
Two reviewers independently followed the data extraction guide­
lines for systematic review and meta-analysis, using pre-designed forms 
to extract and summarize relevant information about eligible studies, 
such as author, year of publication, country, source of the sample, 
sample size, age, WCS assessment, depression assessment tool, out­
comes, covariates, main findings, and subgroup findings. In cases of 
disagreement, the opinion of a third reviewer was sought to reach a 
consensus.
2.5. Risk of bias assessment
Two researchers evaluated the risk of bias in the included studies 
using the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) quality 
assessment tool and cross-checked the results (Zeng et al., 2015). The 
tool includes 11 entries to systematically assess the quality of cross- 
sectional studies. According to the scoring criteria, 0 to 3 was catego­
rized as low quality, 4 to 7 as moderate quality, and 8 to 11 as high 
quality. By using this tool, we were able to comprehensively assess the 
quality of the included studies and ensure the reliability of the analyzed 
results.
2.6. Statistical analysis
In this study, we will assess the overall relationship between WCS 
and depression risk through meta-analysis. Odds ratios (ORs) and 95 % 
confidence intervals (CIs) will be extracted from the included studies. 
The level of heterogeneity will be assessed using the I2 statistic. If I2 <
50 %, a fixed-effects model will be used; if I2 > 50 %, a random-effects 
model will be applied. For studies reporting multiple ORs for different 
WCS durations, OR values with statistical significance (P < 0.05) were 
prioritized for inclusion in the overall quantitative analysis. Addition­
ally, subgroup analyses will be conducted on all reported WCS durations 
to explore the relationship between different WCS durations and 
depression risk. Where data are insufficient for quantitative subgroup 
analyses, the relationship between WCS and depression risk across 
different groups will be summarized qualitatively. Publication bias will 
be assessed using funnel plots and Egger's test. Sensitivity analysis will 
involve excluding each study individually and recalculating the com­
bined effect size to ensure robustness and explore potential biases.

3.1. Literature search process and results
A total of 836 relevant studies were retrieved from the initial search 
through electronic and manual searches, of which 97 were duplicates 
and 713 unrelated studies were excluded after reading the titles and 
abstracts. After screening eligible articles by reading the full text, ten 
studies (Kim et al., 2021; Koo et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022; Liu et al.,

3.2. Basic characteristics of included studies
This systematic review included ten cross-sectional studies, 
comprising a total of 326,871 participants. The studies were published 
between 2019 and 2024, with sample sizes ranging from 1836 to 
270,619 participants. The majority of the studies utilized the Sleep 
Disorders Questionnaire (SDQ) or the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index 
(PSQI) to assess WCS. Depression was evaluated using the Patient Health 
Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9), the Center for Epidemiologic Studies 
Depression Scale (CES-D), or the Beck Depression Inventory (BDI). 
Additionally, two studies employed the anxiety/depression domain of 
the EQ-5D from the Health-Related Quality of Life (HRQoL) instrument. 
Commonly reported covariates included age, sex, and alcohol con­
sumption. The key characteristics of the included studies are summa­
rized in Table 1.
3.3. Risk of bias
We evaluated the quality of the included studies, all of which 
received a score of 7 or higher, indicating that they were of high quality. 
The specific scores for all included observational studies are provided in 
Table 2.
3.4. Results of meta-analysis
Ten observational studies were included in this analysis. Two studies 
were excluded from the quantitative analysis due to their focus on 
anxiety/depression rather than depression alone. Meta-analysis was 
conducted on data from the remaining eight studies. The results showed 
a significant association between WCS and reduced depression risk (OR 
= 0.80, 95 % CI: 0.68–0.90, P = 0.013), suggesting a protective effect of 
WCS (Fig. 2). Regarding publication bias, the funnel plot showed 
asymmetry (Fig. 3), and Egger's test was significant (P = 0.009), indi­
cating potential publication bias. However, no additional studies were 
identified using the Trim and Fill method, suggesting that data were 
insufficient for adjustment. Sensitivity analysis revealed that excluding 
any single study did not significantly alter the overall effect size, con­
firming the robustness of the results (Appendix Fig. A.2).

3.6. Qualitative analysis
Due to the inability to perform quantitative subgroup analyses, the 
main findings of the included studies are presented qualitatively as 
follows:
Sex: (Zheng et al., 2024) found that WCS was significantly negatively 
associated with depression risk in men (OR = 0.70; 95 % CI: 0.05–0.99). 
Similar results were reported by (Luo et al., 2024) and (Liu et al., 2024a, 
2024b) (male OR = 0.63; 95 % CI: 0.47–0.83), where WCS was signif­
icantly negatively associated with depressive symptoms in men. In 
contrast, (Lee et al., 2022) reported that a ratio of weekend sleep time to 
weekday sleep time >1.5 in women was associated with increased 
depressive symptoms. Similarly, (Oh et al., 2023) found that female 
WCS was positively associated with the anxiety/depression dimension of 
the HRQoL.
Age: (Zheng et al., 2024) noted a significant negative correlation in 
the middle-aged 40–60-year-old group (OR = 0.57; 95 % CI: 0.40–0.81). 
(Luo et al., 2024) noted that WCS was more negatively correlated with 
the risk of depression for those younger than 65 years of age. (Park et al., 
2022) for the 35–65-year-old group reached a similar conclusion, with a 
significantly increased risk of depression in the non-WCS group (OR =
2.28; 95 % CI: 1.31–3.98) (Koo et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022; Wang et al., 
2021). Conducted studies in adolescent populations showed that sus­
tained WCS was associated with a lower risk of depressive moods.
Weekday sleep duration: (Zheng et al., 2024) noted that in the 
group with a weekday sleep duration of <7 h, WCS was significantly 
negatively associated with depression compared to the non-WCS group. 
(Luo et al., 2024) observed that WCS was more strongly negatively 
associated with the risk of depression in individuals with shorter 
weekday sleep durations. (Park et al., 2022) found a benefit of WCS on 
depression risk only in the group with a weekday sleep duration of <6 h, 
with no significant association in the other groups. (Lee et al., 2022) 
noted that adolescents with a weekday sleep duration of <5 h and a WCS 
ratio < 1.00 exhibited higher levels of depressive symptoms and suicidal 
ideation.

4. Discussion
This study is the first to systematically summarize the relationship 
between WCS and depression risk, incorporating ten cross-sectional 
studies with a total of 326,871 participants. Pooled analyses revealed 
a significant negative association between WCS and depression risk (OR 
= 0.80, 95 % CI: 0.68–0.90), suggesting that WCS may offer some pro­
tective effect against depression. Subgroup analyses further showed that 
a moderate amount of WCS (0–2 h) was significantly associated with a 
reduction in depression risk, while WCS durations longer than 2 h did 
not show a significant protective effect, suggesting that extended WCS 
may have a limited protective effect or even an adverse impact on 
depression.
WCS mitigates the negative effects of workday sleep deprivation by 
increasing slow-wave and REM sleep, which helps regulate mood and 
relieve stress (Horne, 2020). This is particularly beneficial for those who 
experience sleep deprivation during the workweek (Muto et al., 2016). 
This physiological mechanism supports the depressive protective effect 
of moderate WCS. In recent years, global organizations such as WHO 
have focused on work-life balance, promoting healthy working hours 
and rest policies, and emphasizing the importance of adequate sleep for 
mental health (Piso et al., 2024; Matsumoto et al., 2023). Particularly in 
Western countries, many studies have examined the negative impact of 
‘social jet lag’ on emotional and mental health (Caliandro et al., 2021).
Social jet lag refers to the significant difference in sleep time and 
patterns between weekdays and weekends (Wittmann et al., 2006). This 
irregular sleep schedule may lead to misalignment of the biological 
clock, which in turn can result in mood swings, cognitive decline, and an 
increased risk of depression (Vrabec et al., 2022). There are mechanistic 
similarities between social jet lag and WCS, as both involve adjustments 
to sleep patterns to restore balance to the biological clock. However, 
long-term studies of social jet lag have shown that frequent sleep pattern 
adjustments can increase mental health problems (Caliandro et al., 
2021). Moderate WCS, as a biorhythmic adjustment strategy, may 
mitigate the negative effects caused by social jet lag and provide some 
protection against mood swings and depression risk (Akerstedt et al., 
2019). However, excessive or long-term reliance on WCS may disrupt 
the biological clock and hinder the establishment of stable sleep pat­
terns, leading to negative effects instead (Gershon et al., 2019).
In addition, the occupational demands of different industries also 
influence the need for and effectiveness of WCS. The healthcare in­
dustry, often challenged by sleep deprivation due to shift work, may 
benefit from moderate WCS to restore energy (Chang and Li, 2022). 
However, persistent disruption of circadian rhythms may still increase 
the risk of depression (Yeo et al., 2024). In the technology industry, 
where sleep deprivation is common due to high-pressure work and 
prolonged screen exposure, moderate WCS may provide some psycho­
logical protection (Sato et al., 2020). This suggests that the effects of 
WCS are influenced by different occupational demands and work envi­
ronments, indicating that WCS as a compensatory mechanism is not 
equally effective across all occupational groups. Therefore, WCS should 
be carefully tailored to individual occupational characteristics, life 
rhythms, and global healthy work style recommendations to maximize 
psychological health and avoid potential negative effects.
Sex showed significant differences in the relationship between WCS 
and depression risk. Studies have indicated that, despite a higher prev­
alence of depression in women, the negative correlation between WCS 
and depression risk is more pronounced in men (Zheng et al., 2024; Luo 
et al., 2024). WCS may affect mental health by modulating hormone 
levels, such as increasing testosterone levels in men, which is thought to 
help mitigate depression to some extent (Leproult and Van Cauter, 
2011). However, men over the age of 60 may not experience significant 
benefits from WCS due to hormonal changes and aging factors. Addi­
tionally, middle-aged men often rely on WCS to make up for sleep debt 
accumulated during the workweek, alleviating the dual stresses associ­
ated with their careers and family responsibilities (Zhao et al., 2024).
In contrast, women tend to have shorter sleep durations, longer WCS, 
and more severe depression on weekdays, possibly due to the com­
pounded pressures and emotional complexities arising from their family 
and professional roles (Caetano et al., 2021). Particularly during the 
luteal phase and menopause, the decline in estrogen levels may increase 
cortisol levels, which in turn heightens stress responses and affects mood 
(Salari et al., 2023). (Lee et al., 2022) noted that weekend-to-weekday 
sleep ratios above 1.5 in adolescent females aged 12–18 years were 
associated with an increase in depression symptoms, suggesting that 
WCS in adolescent females may not alleviate depression and could even 
exacerbate mood problems. Similarly, (Oh et al., 2023) found that 
women's WCS was positively associated with the anxiety/depression 
dimension of HRQoL, further highlighting the complexity of women's 
mental health challenges. These findings emphasize the variability of 
WCS effects across sexes, but further validation is needed in different 
cultural contexts, given the diverse sources of study samples.
The effect of WCS on depression risk varies across different age 
groups. Middle-aged individuals often experience sleep deprivation due 
to work and family pressures (Yoshiike et al., 2023). A moderate amount 
of WCS can act as a compensatory mechanism, helping restore energy 
and alleviate depression and other negative emotions. Adolescents, in a 
rapid stage of physical and cognitive development, have a higher need 
for sleep. In addition to supporting physical growth and cognitive 
functioning, they must also manage academic pressures and social de­
mands (Tonetti et al., 2022). WCS can help adolescents reduce the risk of 
depressive symptoms caused by weekday sleep deprivation. However, 
older adults have difficulty adapting to sudden changes in their sleep 
schedules as the regulation of their circadian rhythm diminishes with 
age (Hoyos et al., 2020). As a result, older adults may not experience the 
same benefits from WCS as younger adults, and the disruption of their 
biological clock may exacerbate sleep problems and depression risk 
(Kim et al., 2021).
Future research should explore the interaction between sex and age 
in the relationship between WCS and depression risk to develop more 
targeted intervention strategies. Longitudinal studies analyzing the 
changes in social roles and health needs with age would provide valu­
able insights for improving mental health support for different groups.
4.1. Limitations
The limitations of this study include the following: ① The association 
between WCS and depression risk showed variability across studies, 
likely due to differences in sample characteristics and potential biases, 
highlighting the need for more standardized and rigorous study designs. 
② All included studies were cross-sectional and relied on self-reported 
sleep data, which is prone to recall bias and subjective interpretation, 
limiting causal inferences. The cross-sectional design also precludes the 
examination of the duration and long-term protective effects of WCS, 
and the strength of its protective effect over time. ③ There was notable 
heterogeneity among studies in terms of diagnostic tools, sample char­
acteristics, and statistical methods, which could affect the robustness of 
the meta-analysis results. Although Egger's test did not show significant 
publication bias, funnel plot asymmetry suggests potential bias. ④ The 
majority of the sample came from the United States and South Korea, 
and the lack of data from other regions (e.g., Europe, Africa, South

5. Conclusion
In summary, there is a potential association between WCS and a 
reduced risk of depression, particularly in alleviating the negative ef­
fects of workday sleep deprivation. To maximize the benefits and 
minimize potential negative effects, it is recommended to maintain a 
moderate WCS duration while establishing and maintaining good daily 
sleep habits. Future studies should explore the causal relationship be­
tween WCS and depression risk through large-sample, multicenter lon­
gitudinal designs, and investigate how factors such as sex, age, and 
lifestyle may moderate this relationship. Such studies will help provide a 
more comprehensive understanding of the mechanisms by which WCS 
affects depression risk, leading to the development of more scientific and 
personalized sleep interventions to improve depressive symptoms and 
enhance mental health.
