ABSTRACT
Background: Systematic reviews on acupuncture for obesity report conflicting findings, and the certainty of this evidence remains
unclear. This umbrella review appraises the evidence to identify which effects on body mass index (BMI) and body weight (BW)
are supported by high-quality findings.
Methods: We conducted an umbrella review by systematically searching PubMed, Embase, and the Cochrane Library through
April 28, 2025 to obtain systematic reviews of randomized controlled trials evaluating acupuncture interventions for obesity. Two
reviewers independently assessed methodological quality using a measurement tool to assess systematic reviews (AMSTAR) and
evidence quality using the Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE). We identified best
evidence from high-quality systematic reviews with high or moderate GRADE ratings.
Results: Our analysis included 22 systematic reviews encompassing 60 meta-analyses. Seventeen reviews (77.3%) achieved high
methodological quality ratings according to AMSTAR criteria. Five meta-analyses provided high-quality evidence consistently
demonstrating significant benefits of acupuncture interventions. The strongest evidence supported acupuncture combined with
lifestyle interventions compared to lifestyle interventions alone for both BMI and BW outcomes. Additional high-quality evidence
demonstrated significant benefits for acupuncture versus no treatment and versus sham acupuncture. Fourteen meta-analyses
provided moderate-quality evidence confirming acupuncture effectiveness, with no significant differences between acupuncture
and pharmaceutical treatments.
Conclusions: Based on high-quality evidence, clinicians can recommend acupuncture to patients with obesity, particularly as
adjunctive therapy to lifestyle interventions. For patients unable to tolerate pharmacological treatments, acupuncture represents
a reasonable alternative. However, optimal benefits require integration with comprehensive lifestyle modifications rather than
standalone use.

Introduction
Obesity has long been recognized as “the single greatest threat
to public health for this century” [1]. In 2013, the American
Medical Association formally decided to recognize obesity as a
complex, chronic disease requiring medical attention [2], further
emphasizing its clinical significance. Obesity ranks among the
leading causes of preventable morbidity and mortality worldwide
[3], and obesity is associated with metabolic disorders such
as hypertension, diabetes, and dyslipidemia as well as cardio-
vascular disease, obstructive sleep apnea, premature physical
maturation, polycystic ovarian syndrome, certain cancers, and
mental health conditions such as anxiety and depression [4,
5]. According to the World Trade Organization Fact Sheet, an
estimated 35 million children under the age of 5 years were
overweight in 2024 [6].
Successful weight loss maintenance can be difficult to achieve
through lifestyle interventions alone [7]. Other methods such
as drugs or surgery have potentially serious adverse events and
often do not show long term benefits [8–10]. Thus, the need for
complementary and alternative medicine approaches on weight
loss is increasing, and acupuncture is one of the most widely
used approaches [11]. Acupuncture is a Traditional Chinese
Medicine therapy that involves penetrating the body’s surface by
one or more needles that encompass manual acupuncture, elec-
troacupuncture or a combination of both [12]. Existing systematic
reviews (SRs) on acupuncture for obesity present conflicting
findings, and critically, the certainty of the evidence supporting
their conclusions has not been systematically appraised [13, 14].
Given this variability, a single, comprehensive, and user-friendly
summary of the best available evidence is urgently needed to
guide clinical decision-making.
Therefore, this umbrella review was conducted to identify the
evidence on acupuncture for obesity and to distinguish outcomes
supported by high-quality evidence, particularly for body mass
index (BMI) and body weight (BW), from those based on less reli-
able findings. To achieve this primary objective, we systematically
evaluated several key domains: the consistency of findings across
the included SRs, the overall methodological quality of the SRs
themselves, the strength of the evidence for each key outcome,
and the resulting clinical applicability of the most robust findings.
2
Methods
This study adhered to the checklist of reporting guideline for
overviews of reviews of healthcare interventions (PRIOR) [15].
The umbrella review was prospectively registered on the Open
Science Framework (https://osf.io/vsjy5).
2.1
Inclusion Criteria
To be included, articles had to be SRs with meta-analyses meeting
the following criteria: (1) Population: The studies focused on
adult or adolescent populations with overweight or obesity.
(2) Intervention: The intervention was any form of needle-
based acupuncture (e.g., manual, electroacupuncture, auricular),
administered either as a standalone therapy or as an adjunct. In
our overview, the term “acupuncture” was used to mean pene-
trating the body’s surface by one or more needles, but this was
not necessarily how the SR authors that have published the SRs
included in this overview may have referred to “acupuncture.” (3)
Comparators: Eligible comparators included a range of controls
such as sham acupuncture, no treatment, pharmacological treat-
ments, or lifestyle interventions. (4) Outcomes: The SRs provided
extractable quantitative data for at least one primary outcome,
specifically BMI or BW. (5) Study design: The included primary
studies were required to be randomized controlled trials (RCTs).
2.2
Exclusion Criteria
Exclusion criteria were (1) protocols; (2) SRs focused on subjects
with serious medical conditions, or drug-induced obesity; (3) SRs
comparing the effects of different acupuncture modalities (e.g.,
manual vs. electroacupuncture); (4) SRs that pooled heteroge-
neous control groups (e.g., sham, drug, no treatment) in a single
meta-analysis; (5) SRs failing to provide the quantitative data; and
(6) guidelines and conference abstracts.
2.3
Search Strategy
PubMed, EMbase, and the Cochrane Library were searched from
inception to April 28, 2025. The search strategy for each database
included a combination of MeSH terms and text word to represent
the definitions of acupuncture, obesity, and SRs. Details on
search strategies could be found in Supplementary Material A.
Additionally, the references of included SRs were also screened
for further relevant SRs to ensure the comprehensiveness of the
search. No publication status restriction was made for selecting
studies.
2.4
Data Collection and Analysis
2.4.1
Studies Selection
Two reviewers independently screened for potential articles
based on title and abstract. All potential studies obtained in full
text were further assessed. Any discrepancies were resolved by
consensus or consultation.
2.4.2
Data Extraction and Management
Using a predefined standardized data collection instrument,
two reviewers independently extracted the data from included
studies. Disagreements were resolved by discussion between the
two reviewers or consulting the third reviewer.
The following data were extracted: title, author, publication year,
country, interventions and comparators, number of included
RCTs and participants, whether meta-analysis was conducted,
outcomes, reported results, and conclusions. In cases of dis-
crepant or missing data across SRs, we extracted data from all
included primary studies and addressed the discrepancies or gaps
by consulting the primary studies cited within the reviews or,
when necessary, searching relevant registries.

2.5
Assessment of Methodological Quality of
Included SRs
Methodological quality of included SRs was evaluated by using a
measurement tool to assess systematic reviews (AMSTAR) tool by
two reviewers independently [16]. Disagreements were resolved
by discussion between the two reviewers or consulting the third
reviewer. The AMSTAR tool comprises 11 items, with each item
scored as “Yes,” “No,” “Can’t Answer,” or “Not Applicable.” A
score of 1 is assigned for each “Yes,” and 0 for all other responses.
The total score ranges from 0 to 11, and the overall quality of the
SRs was classified into three levels: high quality (8–11 points),
medium quality (4–7 points), and low quality (0–3 points).
2.6
Quality of Evidence for Each Comparison
The Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development,
and Evaluation (GRADE) tool was used to indicate the quality
of the evidence for each comparison [17]. The GRADE level
was determined based on information provided in the included
SRs, supplemented by examination of primary studies when
necessary. Evidence was systematically downgraded for serious
limitations in risk of bias (> 25% of studies with high risk),
inconsistency (I2 > 50% with unexplained heterogeneity), indi-
rectness (population / intervention / outcome differences from
question), imprecision (confidence intervals crossing clinical
decision thresholds), and publication bias (funnel plot asymmetry
or significant Egger’s tests as reported in SRs). The assess-
ment results were classified as high-, moderate- (downgraded
one level), low- (downgraded two levels), or very-low-quality
(downgraded three or more levels) evidence.
2.7
Data Synthesis
We presented data as a narrative synthesis. Quantitative analysis
was not performed because of the overlaps between the RCTs
included in the SRs. We defined SRs with a high-quality AMSTAR
score as high-quality SRs. Subsequently, meta-analyses within
these high-quality SRs that were rated as having high or moderate
certainty by GRADE were considered to represent the best
evidence. To better visualize the results, we generated bubble plot
for the outcomes supported by the best evidence.
To standardize the direction of effect sizes for meta-analysis, we
multiplied the mean differences by –1 for studies that reported
continuous outcomes as a positive value for improvement (e.g.,
amount of reduction). The corresponding 95% confidence inter-
vals were transformed accordingly by reversing their upper and
lower bounds. This ensured that a negative effect size consistently
represented a favorable outcome for the acupuncture group
across all pooled studies.
3
Results
3.1
Study Selection
The comprehensive literature search and selection process are
illustrated in Figure 1. Initially, 434 citations were identified for
screening. After 124 duplicate titles were removed, unique records
310 remained for screening. Then, 244 were excluded based on
title and abstract screening, leaving 66 publications for full text
assessments. Finally, 44 full-text articles were excluded (a list of
these studies and the specific reasons for exclusion are provided
in Table S1). A total of 22 SRs, encompassing 60 meta-analyses,
were included in this umbrella review.
3.2
Characteristics
The characteristics of the 22 included SRs are summarized in
Table 1. Additional characteristics are described in Table S2. The
SRs were published between 2009 and 2024, with the majority (n
= 16, 72.7%) published after 2015, reflecting the growing interest
in this research area. The SRs originated from various countries,
with China contributing the highest number of studies (n = 14,
63.6%), followed by South Korea (n = 3, 13.6%). The number
of included RCTs in individual SRs ranged from 1 to 22, with
participant numbers ranging from 60 to 2814. All SRs conducted
meta-analyses. The included SRs examined various acupuncture
interventions, including manual acupuncture, electroacupunc-
ture, auricular acupuncture, acupoint catgut embedding, and
pharmacopuncture.
3.3
Methodological Quality Assessment
The methodological quality of included SRs assessed using the
AMSTAR tool is presented in Table 2. Among the 22 SRs, 17
(77.3%) were rated as high quality, and the remaining 5 (22.7%)
were rated as medium quality. No SRs were assessed as low
quality. The total AMSTAR scores ranged from 5 to 11, with a mean
score of 8.5.
All (100%) of the included SRs provided the characteristics of
their included studies (Item 6) and documented the scientific
quality assessment of these studies (Item 7). Furthermore, a high
proportion of SRs utilized appropriate methods for combining
findings (100%, Item 9), conducted duplicate study selection and
data extraction (90.9%, Item 2), and considered the scientific
quality of included studies when formulating conclusions (90.9%,
Item 8) (Figure S1).
3.4
Quality of Evidence Assessment
A comprehensive summary of effect sizes, confidence intervals,
heterogeneity measures, and corresponding GRADE certainty
levels are presented in Table S3.

was more effective than lifestyle interventions alone for reducing

3.4.2
Moderate-Quality Evidence
Fourteen meta-analyses provided moderate-quality evidence
(Figure 3, Table S3), revealing a complex but generally posi-
tive picture of acupuncture’s effectiveness. The most frequently
assessed comparison was acupuncture versus a sham control. For
instance, Zhang (2018) [29] found that acupuncture significantly
reduced both BMI (MD = –1.22 kg/m2, 95% CI [–1.87, –0.56])
and BW (MD = –1.54 kg, 95% CI [–2.98, –0.11]). Similarly, Zhong
(2020) [31] reported significant decreases in both BMI (MD = –
1.00 kg/m2, 95% CI [–1.40, –0.60]) and BW (MD = –1.85 kg, 95%
CI [–2.88, –0.82]). The evidence also supported specific forms
like auriculotherapy over no treatment, with Chen (2022) [35]
reporting substantial reductions in BMI (MD = –1.08 kg/m2, 95%
CI [–1.29, –0.86]) and BW (MD = –2.85 kg, 95% CI [–3.34, –
2.35]). However, the evidence was not uniformly positive; when
compared against pharmacological treatments, analyses by Guo
(2015) [32] found no statistically significant difference for either
BMI or BW.

3.4.3
Low- and Very-Low-Quality Evidence
Twenty-six meta-analyses were classified as providing low-
quality evidence, and 15 provided very-low-quality evidence
(Figures S2 and S3, Table S3). These meta-analyses encom-
passed a wide range of comparisons, including acupuncture
versus sham controls, lifestyle modifications, exercise, or phar-
macological treatments. The findings within this group were
largely inconsistent and often contradictory. While some anal-
yses suggested minor benefits, many others reported no sta-
tistically significant differences between acupuncture and con-
trol conditions for either BMI or BW. Given the significant
methodological limitations, high risk of bias, and considerable
heterogeneity reported in these studies, no reliable conclu-
sions could be drawn from this body of evidence. Therefore,
these findings were considered secondary to the higher-quality
evidence.
4
Discussion
This comprehensive umbrella review appraised and summa-
rized evidence from 22 SRs and 60 meta-analyses to evaluate
acupuncture’s clinical effectiveness for obesity treatment. High-
certainty evidence supports the use of acupuncture as an adjunct
to lifestyle interventions. Specifically, one high-quality meta-
analysis found that this combination significantly reduced BMI
by –2.11 kg/m2 and BW by –4.37 kg compared to lifestyle alone, a
result likely attributable to neurobiological mechanisms such as
hypothalamic modulation and enhanced leptin sensitivity [24].
Furthermore, moderate-certainty evidence confirms the benefits
of acupuncture versus sham or no treatment, while showing
no significant differences when compared to pharmacological
treatments. Clinically, these findings position acupuncture as
a valuable adjunct to lifestyle interventions for enhancing out-
comes. It also serves as a viable alternative for patients intolerant

The biological plausibility of our effectiveness findings is substan-
tially corroborated by emerging mechanistic research revealing
multi-targeted therapeutic pathways through which acupuncture
exerts its influence on obesity pathophysiology. The evidence
demonstrates sophisticated neurobiological mechanisms involv-
ing hypothalamic regulation, particularly targeting the arcuate
nucleus where acupuncture modulates competing orexigenic
and anorexigenic neuronal populations [38]. Functional mag-
netic resonance imaging studies consistently show activation
in brain regions controlling appetite and metabolism, including
the basal ganglia, brainstem, and cerebellum, providing neu-
roimaging validation for acupuncture’s central nervous system
effects [39].
Hormonal mechanisms offer particularly compelling explana-
tions for clinical effectiveness. Leptin sensitivity enhancement
through improved receptor binding and expression provides a
mechanistic basis for appetite regulation improvements observed
in clinical trials [40]. The context-dependent effects on ghre-
lin, where acupuncture can both increase and decrease levels
depending on baseline status, suggest sophisticated regulatory
mechanisms that help restore normal appetite signaling in obesity
[38]. Insulin sensitivity improvements through AMPK activation
and enhanced glucose uptake mechanisms support the benefits
observed in high-certainty evidence [41]. Recent research has
identified acupuncture’s ability to promote white adipose tissue
browning through UCP1 induction and thermogenic capacity
enhancement [39]. This “beiging” effect represents a revolution-
ary mechanism that converts energy-storing adipocytes to energy-
releasing thermogenic cells [42], providing a strong biological
foundation for the sustained weight loss effects in higher-quality
studies.
Common methodological weaknesses, derived from AMSTAR
domain ratings, included Item 5 (providing a list of included
and excluded studies, met by only 18.18% of SRs), Item 1 (a
priori design or protocol, 45.45%), Item 4 (inclusion of gray
literature, 63.64%), and Item 3 (comprehensive literature search,
68.18%). Low adherence to Item 5, where only four SRs provided
full lists, limits transparency and reproducibility, potentially
obscuring selection bias. The lack of a priori protocols (Item 1,
unmet by 12 SRs) raises concerns about selective reporting, as
unregistered methods may allow post hoc adjustments. Failure
to include gray literature (Item 4, unmet by eight SRs) increases
publication bias risk, potentially overestimating acupuncture’s
effects. Incomplete search strategies (Item 3, unmet by seven
SRs) may miss relevant studies, reducing evidence comprehen-
siveness. These weaknesses, particularly in transparency and
bias minimization, likely contributed to the downgrading of
the included meta-analyses to low or very low certainty in
GRADE assessments, underscoring the need for stricter adher-
ence to AMSTAR standards in future SRs to enhance evidence
reliability.

Our comprehensive analysis reveals both consistencies and
contradictions across the 60 meta-analyses that warrant careful
consideration. Several key consistencies across different quality
levels are observed. The most robust and consistent finding is
that acupuncture combined with lifestyle interventions yields
significantly greater reductions in both BMI and BW compared
to lifestyle interventions alone. Furthermore, the evidence con-
sistently indicates that acupuncture is therapeutically superior
to no treatment. In contrast, several areas of divergence and
contradiction emerged from the evidence. The most prominent
is the effect of acupuncture versus sham controls. While many
analyses reported a statistically significant, albeit modest, benefit
for true acupuncture, a substantial number of studies across all
quality levels found no significant difference. This inconsistency
suggests that the specific effects of acupuncture beyond placebo
are variable and may be influenced by the type of sham control
used. The long-term efficacy of acupuncture for weight main-
tenance remains largely unaddressed within the current body
of evidence. The vast majority of included studies focused on
short-term outcomes (weeks to months), leaving a significant gap
in our understanding of whether the benefits of acupuncture
persist after treatment cessation. The predominance of studies
from East Asian populations limits generalizability, highlighting
the need for more diverse geographical representation in future
research. Future high-quality RCTs should therefore prioritize
standardized and credible sham controls, direct comparisons
with active medications, and incorporate long-term follow-up
periods to resolve these crucial uncertainties.
The most urgent priority for advancing acupuncture obesity
research is developing evidence-based standardization proto-
cols that balance intervention reproducibility with traditional
individualized treatment approaches [43]. Recent expert consen-
sus recommends implementing STRICTA 2010 guidelines with
mandatory reporting of all intervention details, from acupuncture
rationale and needling specifics to practitioner qualifications and
treatment settings [44]. Outcome measurement standardization
requires consensus on primary endpoints, with BW reduction (≥
2 kg) and BMI reduction (≥1.0 kg/m2) emerging as the most
clinically meaningful targets. Compared to earlier overviews [45],
our umbrella review incorporates more recently published high-
quality SRs and applies rigorous evidence-grading, thus providing
the most current and reliable summary for clinical practice.
This umbrella review has several limitations. First, the quality of
an umbrella review is inherently dependent t upon the quality
of the SRs it includes. Although we used the validated AMSTAR
tool for quality assessment, persistent issues in the primary RCTs
can still impact the overall certainty of the evidence. Second,
there was significant heterogeneity across the included SRs
regarding acupuncture interventions (e.g., manual acupuncture,
electroacupuncture, auricular acupuncture, catgut embedding)
and control groups. This variability makes direct comparisons
challenging and is why we refrained from conducting a new
meta-analysis. Third, we did not report the results of different

Based on the highest-quality evidence, clinicians can recommend
acupuncture to patients with obesity, particularly as an effective
adjunctive therapy to lifestyle interventions such as diet and
exercise. Furthermore, for patients who are unable to tolerate or
are unwilling to use pharmacological treatments, acupuncture
presents a reasonable alternative, supported by moderate-quality
evidence. For successful clinical application, however, it is crucial
to communicate to patients that acupuncture is not a standalone
“magic bullet” for weight loss; its optimal and most durable
benefits are realized through a sustained commitment to a
healthy lifestyle. While further high-quality research is needed
to refine protocols and confirm long-term efficacy, the current
evidence strongly supports the integration of acupuncture into
comprehensive weight management strategies.
